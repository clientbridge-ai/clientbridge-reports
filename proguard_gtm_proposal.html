Executive Summary

The Message Isn't the Problem.
The List Is.

ProGuard Building is the #1 supplier of GE Silicone roof coatings in the U.S., with products distributed across 20+ regional territories. The core value proposition — restore instead of re-roof, one-coat application, no primer, lowest cost per applied square — is strong. The problem is who hears it and when.

Most outreach in this space targets roofing contractors based on company size or geography. Every competitor with a rep team does the same. The result: undifferentiated cold calls to contractors who aren't in a buying moment, selling on spec sheets that all look alike.

"The coating sells itself to a facility manager staring at a $400K replacement bid. The job is finding them at that exact moment — not blasting a list of roofing contractors who last bought six months ago."

This proposal identifies three Pain-Qualified Segment (PQS) scenarios and three Permissionless Value Proposition (PVP) concepts that reach ProGuard's highest-value buyers — facility managers, commercial property owners, and roofing contractors — at the specific moments when they are most likely to act.

Company Intelligence

What ProGuard Building Actually Sells

ProGuard Building (Santa Fe Springs, CA) is a specialty roofing products distributor — not a contractor. Their core business is channel sales: getting roofing contractors to specify and use GE Enduris silicone coatings and ProGuard acrylic coatings instead of competing products (or recommending full replacement). End customers are commercial facilities, industrial buildings, schools, government properties, and large retail/warehouse properties.

Primary Buyers (End Customers)
Facility managers, commercial property owners, building operations directors at institutions with 10,000–200,000 sq ft flat/low-slope roofs
Channel Customers (Who Orders)
Commercial roofing contractors, restoration specialists, coating-focused subcontractors in TX, CA, LA, HI, and other ProGuard distribution territories
Best-Fit Projects
Roofs 10–25 years old showing minor deterioration, ponding, or minor leaks where the membrane is structurally intact — coating candidates, not tear-offs
Core Competitive Angle
GE Enduris requires no primer, single-coat application, no MEKO off-gas, 50+ year manufacturer pedigree — specs competitors can't match at the same price point
Target Personas

Two Distinct Buyer Types,
Two Very Different Conversations

Persona A — The Facility Manager / Director of Operations at a commercial building, manufacturing plant, school district, or logistics facility. Roof issues show up as work order backlogs, emergency repair costs, and budget surprises. They have one roof they're watching. The trigger for action: a leak, a pending inspection, a capital budget cycle, or a re-roofing bid that came in much higher than expected.

Persona B — The Coating-Capable Roofing Contractor who already does restoration work or wants to add it. They're looking for a product with better margins, better specs, and a distributor who will actually support the job. Their trigger: a large coating job coming up, a GE certification opportunity, or losing a spec comparison to another product.

Current State Analysis

What the Current GTM Playbook Looks Like

The standard approach for a specialty building products distributor in 2026:

  • Cold call roofing contractors from a purchased list, filtered by geography
  • Email campaigns to facility managers with product spec sheets and a "call us for a free sample" CTA
  • Trade show presence at regional roofing associations
  • Rep-driven relationships with existing contractor accounts

These tactics reach people who are not yet in a buying moment. The facility manager who gets an email about silicone coatings in October isn't thinking about their roof. The one getting three replacement bids in March is. The difference isn't the message — it's the timing and the list.

80% of commercial roofs are replaced before they need to be. The building owner who just got a $380,000 tear-off bid hasn't heard that a coating might solve the same problem for $65,000. That's the conversation ProGuard should be having — but only the right 1% of facility managers is in that moment at any given time.

Pain-Qualified Segments

Three PQS Scenarios Built on
Verifiable Public Signals

Each scenario identifies a specific, verifiable situation where a facility manager or property owner is actively experiencing roof-related pain — and is reachable before a competitor claims the job.

PQS 01
Commercial Buildings That Just Filed for Roofing Permits (Replacement Intent)
Data Recipe
Municipal building permit databases (state/county open data portals) → filter for commercial roofing permits, type: "re-roof" or "roof replacement" → cross-reference property age via assessor records → identify buildings where coating was a viable alternative
Pain Signal
Owner/FM filed for a full replacement permit — they're in procurement mode and likely just got their first expensive bid
Why Now
Permit is pulled but contract likely not yet signed — there's a 2–6 week window before they commit to a contractor
Target
Commercial facilities 15,000–200,000 sq ft with flat/low-slope roofs aged 10–20 years in ProGuard distribution territories
Public Data Source
County assessor sites, state open data portals (e.g., Texas DPS permit search, LA City LADBS permit portal), BuildZoom API
Building Permits Assessor Records BuildZoom TX/CA/LA Territories

Why Now

Target
School districts, county government buildings, large retail chains (CA/TX focus) with 50,000+ sq ft of flat commercial roof

Permissionless Value Propositions

Three PVP Concepts That Deliver
Intelligence Before Asking for Anything

Each PVP concept delivers a piece of analysis or intelligence that is immediately useful to the recipient — whether or not they ever engage ProGuard. The goal is to create a message so useful they'd forward it to a colleague.

PVP 01
The "Your Roof Age vs. Coating Window" Analysis
Data Recipe
County assessor records (building age, last permit) + Google Maps satellite imagery (roof type/condition visual) + local climate data (UV index, precipitation patterns for territory)

Core Insight: There's a specific age window — roughly years 10–18 for most commercial flat roof systems — where coating is viable and cost-effective. Before year 10, it's not needed. After year 20, replacement is often more prudent. Facility managers don't know where their roof sits in this window. ProGuard does, with public data.

Target Persona: Facility Manager or Director of Operations, commercial/industrial buildings 15,000–100,000 sq ft, buildings constructed 2000–2014 (in the coating-eligible age band).

Outreach Message

Subject: [Property address] — your roof coating window Based on public permit and assessor records, your facility at [address] has a flat/low-slope roof installed around [year]. That puts it in what we'd call the optimal coating window — old enough to benefit from restoration, not yet at the point where underlying system integrity becomes a question. Coatings applied in this window typically add 15–20 years of service life at 20–30% of what a tear-off runs in your market. Waiting another 3–5 years significantly narrows your options. I put together a one-page comparison of coating vs. replacement cost ranges for [city] properties in your roof type — want me to send it over?

PVP 02
The "Coating vs. Replacement Cost Map" for Roofing Contractors
Data Recipe
RSMeans regional cost data (public annual reports) + local contractor job posting rates (Indeed/LinkedIn) + ProGuard regional material pricing + recent permit valuations from municipal databases

Core Insight: Most roofing contractors quote replacement jobs because that's where they know the margin. But in the current labor market, coating jobs — properly spec'd with single-coat silicone — have higher gross margin per day of crew time than tear-offs in most metro markets. This analysis makes that case with current numbers, specific to the contractor's territory.

Target Persona: Owner or estimator at a commercial roofing company doing $2M–$15M annually, primarily replacement-focused, in ProGuard distribution territory.

Outreach Message

At current market pricing for that scope, gross margin per crew day runs about 30–40% higher than a comparable TPO replacement. I put together a one-page breakdown comparing coating vs. replacement margins for jobs in your size range in [metro area]. Happy to send it — no pitch attached, just the numbers. Would that be useful?

PVP 03
The "Competitor Product Spec Comparison" for Contractors at Bid Time
Data Recipe
Manufacturer TDS (Technical Data Sheets, publicly available) for GacoFlex, Conklin, GAF, Tremco + published warranty terms + material cost per sq ft (distributor pricing, publicly quoted) + application requirement comparison

Core Insight: When a facility manager gets competing coating bids, the spec sheets all look similar. But the application requirements are dramatically different — primer requirements, number of coats, cure time, ponding water performance, and warranty terms vary widely. ProGuard can deliver a side-by-side comparison that makes GE Enduris's advantages immediately visible — and gives the contractor a tool to use when their customer asks "why this product?"

Target Persona: Commercial roofing contractor estimator or owner preparing a coating bid where the end customer is comparing multiple proposals. Trigger: contractor has an active bid in progress (visible from permit activity or active job postings).

Outreach Message

Subject: Coating spec comparison — useful if you're bidding against Gaco or Conklin If you're putting together a coating bid where the customer is comparing products, I put together a side-by-side spec comparison — GE Enduris vs. GacoFlex S20 vs. Conklin — covering primer requirements, coat count, ponding water rating, warranty structure, and cost per square at current pricing. It's built to hand directly to a facility manager who asks "why GE?" Short answer: no primer, one coat, no MEKO, 50+ year manufacturer. Longer answer is in the comparison. Want me to send the PDF? Takes 30 seconds to review.

Strategic Advantage

The Data Moat ProGuard Can Build

Why These Signals Create Durable Competitive Advantage

ProGuard's competitors — other silicone distributors, acrylic coating suppliers, full-line roofing distributors — are reaching roofing contractors through the same channels: trade shows, reps, cold calls, spec sheets. None of them are identifying facility managers at the permit stage, or contractors hiring for restoration work, or institutions inside post-audit budget cycles.

Once ProGuard builds the workflow to monitor these signals consistently, the advantage compounds. Each job won through permit monitoring adds a reference property in that market. Each contractor converted through hiring signal monitoring reduces competitor share in that territory. The effort to build these data recipes is high; the effort to maintain them is low.

Competitors who continue outreach based on geography and company size don't know what they're missing. That gap is the moat.

Success Metrics

How to Measure What's Working

  • Reply Rate on PQS Outreach — Target: 8–15% (vs. industry standard 1–3% for cold outreach in construction/facilities). A reply rate below 5% signals the signal or the message needs refinement, not more volume.
  • PVP Asset Requests — Number of facility managers or contractors who request the comparison PDF or margin analysis. Each request is a qualified conversation, not a cold follow-up.
  • Permit-to-Inspection Conversion — Of facility managers reached via permit signal, how many request a free inspection referral to a ProGuard contractor? This is the clearest pipeline signal.
  • Contractor Certification Rate — Of contractors reached via hiring signal, how many complete GE certification within 90 days? This measures downstream product adoption, not just outreach metrics.
  • Territory Coverage Rate — By month 4, how many of ProGuard's 20+ territories have an active signal monitoring workflow in place? Coverage is the scale metric.
