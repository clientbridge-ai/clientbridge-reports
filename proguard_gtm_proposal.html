Executive Summary

The Message Isn't the Problem.
The List Is.

ProGuard Building is the #1 supplier of GE Silicone roof coatings in the U.S., with products distributed across 20+ regional territories. The core value proposition — restore instead of re-roof, one-coat application, no primer, lowest cost per applied square — is strong. The problem is who hears it and when.

Most outreach in this space targets roofing contractors based on company size or geography. Every competitor with a rep team does the same. The result: undifferentiated cold calls to contractors who aren't in a buying moment, selling on spec sheets that all look alike.

"The coating sells itself to a facility manager staring at a $400K replacement bid. The job is finding them at that exact moment — not blasting a list of roofing contractors who last bought six months ago."

This proposal — updated with validated market research — identifies five Pain-Qualified Segment (PQS) scenarios and four Permissionless Value Proposition (PVP) concepts across a broader set of buyer types than the original draft. Two new PQS scenarios (solar permit activity, commercial real estate transactions) and one new PVP (PACE financing intelligence) were added based on research confirming accessible signals and strong buyer intent. A fourth buyer type — regional property management companies — is identified as a channel opportunity with a distinct outreach approach.

Company Intelligence

What ProGuard Building Actually Sells

ProGuard Building (Santa Fe Springs, CA) is a specialty roofing products distributor — not a contractor. Their core business is channel sales: getting roofing contractors to specify and use GE Enduris silicone coatings and ProGuard acrylic coatings instead of competing products (or recommending full replacement). End customers are commercial facilities, industrial buildings, schools, government properties, and large retail/warehouse properties.

Primary Buyers (End Customers)
Facility managers, commercial property owners, building operations directors at institutions with 10,000–200,000 sq ft flat/low-slope roofs
Channel Customers (Who Orders)
Commercial roofing contractors, restoration specialists, coating-focused subcontractors in TX, CA, LA, HI, and other ProGuard distribution territories
Best-Fit Projects
Roofs 10–20 years old showing minor deterioration, ponding, or minor leaks where the membrane is structurally intact — coating candidates, not tear-offs. Industry data confirms ~60% of commercial flat roofs are coating-viable at some point in their service life.
Core Competitive Angle
GE Enduris requires no primer, single-coat application, no MEKO off-gas, 50+ year manufacturer pedigree — specs competitors (GacoFlex, Conklin, GAF, Tremco) can't match at the same price point
Market Context

The Market ProGuard Is Selling Into

The U.S. commercial roof coating market sits in the low-to-mid single-digit billions, growing at 4–6% annually. Silicone and acrylic are the dominant chemistries. The restoration-vs-replacement decision point — where ProGuard's message is most relevant — is specifically concentrated in commercial flat and low-slope roofs in the 10–20 year age band. In high-growth metros like Houston, Dallas, Los Angeles, and the Inland Empire, the 2006–2016 construction wave means a large portion of commercial stock is entering that window right now.

Industry data confirms that 80% of commercial roofs are replaced before they need to be. That's ProGuard's entire market opportunity — and it's a targeting problem, not a product problem.

Current State Analysis

What the Current GTM Playbook Looks Like

The standard approach for a specialty building products distributor in 2026:

  • Cold call roofing contractors from a purchased list, filtered by geography
  • Email campaigns to facility managers with product spec sheets and a "call us for a free sample" CTA
  • Trade show presence at regional roofing associations
  • Rep-driven relationships with existing contractor accounts

These tactics reach people who are not yet in a buying moment. The facility manager who gets an email about silicone coatings in October isn't thinking about their roof. The one getting three replacement bids in March is. The difference isn't the message — it's the timing and the list.

53% of commercial roofing contractors are involved in coatings — but coatings represent only 6% of their average revenue. The gap between involvement and commitment is exactly where ProGuard's targeting should focus.

Permissionless Value Propositions

Four PVP Concepts That Deliver
Intelligence Before Asking for Anything

Each PVP concept delivers a piece of analysis or intelligence that is immediately useful to the recipient — whether or not they ever engage ProGuard. The goal is to create a message so useful they'd forward it to a colleague. PVP 4 is new, targeting property owners in PACE financing conversations.

PVP 01
The "Your Roof Age vs. Coating Window" Analysis
Data Recipe
County assessor records (building age, last permit) + Google Maps satellite imagery (roof type/condition visual) + local climate data (UV index, precipitation patterns for territory)

Core Insight: There's a specific age window — roughly years 10–18 for most commercial flat roof systems — where coating is viable and cost-effective. Before year 10, it's not needed. After year 20, replacement is often more prudent. Facility managers don't know where their roof sits in this window. ProGuard does, with public data.

Target Persona: Facility Manager or Director of Operations, commercial/industrial buildings 15,000–100,000 sq ft, buildings constructed 2000–2014 (in the coating-eligible age band).

New Buyer Type — Research Finding

Property Management Companies:
Portfolio-Scale Opportunity

Research confirmed a buyer type not addressed in the original proposal: regional and national property management companies managing portfolios of commercial or industrial properties across multiple buildings. These firms make roofing decisions centrally — through a Director of Facilities, VP of Asset Management, or Regional Engineering head — rather than building-by-building.

Why This Buyer Type Is Different

A single facility manager controls one roof. A property management company controls 20–200 roofs across a portfolio. One converted relationship at a regional PM firm can produce multiple coating projects per year across their managed properties — all using the same preferred vendor and spec.

These firms run formal annual improvement plans and capital replacement schedules (aligned with IFMA guidance). Roof condition is a line item in their asset management system. The decision-maker is evaluating cost-per-building across the portfolio, not just one property's replacement quote.

Targeting approach: BOMA (Building Owners and Managers Association) and NAIOP member directories in ProGuard territories provide direct access to portfolio-level facilities contacts. LinkedIn search for "Director of Facilities" or "VP Asset Management" at property management companies with 10+ managed commercial properties in TX/CA/LA. The outreach angle is a portfolio-wide "Restore vs. Replace" cost baseline analysis — a PVP deliverable that requires their building list as input and returns a prioritized maintenance schedule.

Key channel note: These firms source vendors through preferred-vendor lists and RFP processes. The goal of first outreach is not to sell product — it's to get onto the preferred vendor list or into their next capital planning cycle. Aligning ProGuard's certified contractors with a PM firm's existing roofing contractor relationships is the most efficient path.

Strategic Advantage

The Data Moat ProGuard Can Build

Why These Signals Create Durable Competitive Advantage

ProGuard's competitors — other silicone distributors, acrylic coating suppliers, full-line roofing distributors — are reaching roofing contractors through the same channels: trade shows, reps, cold calls, spec sheets. None of them are identifying facility managers at the permit stage, intercepting solar project timelines, or reaching commercial property sellers before their PCA.

The five PQS signals in this proposal operate at different points in the buyer journey — from years before a decision (PVP 1, roof age analysis) to weeks before a contract is signed (PQS 1, permit signal) to a specific project deadline (PQS 4, solar install timing). Covering multiple entry points means ProGuard captures buyers at whichever moment they become ready, regardless of which trigger fires first.

The effort to identify and operationalize these data recipes is high. The effort to run them at scale once built is low. Competitors who rely on geography and firmographics don't know what they're missing — and that gap is the moat.

Success Metrics

How to Measure What's Working

  • Reply Rate on PQS Outreach — Target: 8–15% (vs. industry standard 1–3% for cold outreach in construction/facilities). A reply rate below 5% signals the signal or the message needs refinement, not more volume.
  • PVP Asset Requests — Number of facility managers, property owners, or contractors who request any of the four PVP deliverables. Each request is a qualified conversation, not a cold follow-up. Track which PVP converts highest by persona type.
  • Permit-to-Inspection Conversion — Of facility managers reached via permit signal (PQS 1), how many request a free inspection referral to a ProGuard contractor? This is the clearest pipeline signal for the core scenario.
  • Solar Signal Response Quality — Track separately from the re-roof permit signal (PQS 4). Solar contacts are earlier in the buyer journey — response rate may be lower but conversation quality higher. A single converted solar project = coating + potential repeat as panels are maintained.
  • Contractor Certification Rate — Of contractors reached via hiring signal (PQS 2), how many complete GE certification within 90 days? Measures downstream product adoption.
  • Territory Coverage Rate — By month 5, how many of ProGuard's 20+ territories have an active signal monitoring workflow in place across at least two signal types? Coverage + signal diversity is the scale metric.
