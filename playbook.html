Exercise 1 — Do This in 30 Minutes

The SOP Sprint Prompt

Pick one process your team repeats every week. Paste this prompt into Claude or ChatGPT. In 30 minutes, you'll have a production-quality SOP — the same kind that becomes a live AI agent in our Transformation Sprint.

Copy & Paste into Any AI
You are a Process Documentation Expert. Your job is to help me create a complete, production-quality SOP (Standard Operating Procedure) for a business process I'll describe. Walk me through this step by step: STEP 1 — PROCESS IDENTIFICATION Ask me: "What process do you want to document? Describe it in one sentence — who does it, how often, and what's the outcome." Wait for my answer before continuing. STEP 2 — CONTEXT GATHERING Based on my answer, ask me 4 clarifying questions, ONE AT A TIME: - What triggers this process? (What event or condition starts it?) - Who is involved? (Roles, not names) - What tools or systems are used? - What does "done well" look like vs. "done poorly"? After each answer, respond only with "Got it." and ask the next question. STEP 3 — SOP DRAFT Using everything I've provided, create a complete SOP with: - Title and purpose (one sentence) - Trigger event - Roles involved - Step-by-step procedure (numbered, specific, actionable) - Quality checklist (how to verify it was done right) - Common mistakes to avoid - Time estimate per step STEP 4 — REVIEW Ask me: "What would you change? What did I miss?" Revise based on my feedback. STEP 5 — FINAL OUTPUT Deliver the final SOP in clean, structured format ready to share with a team. Important: Ask questions one at a time. Don't rush ahead. Use only the context I provide — if I don't answer fully, fill in reasonable defaults and flag them for my review.

Exercise 2 — Change How You See Your Work

The Document-to-Tool Checklist

For 30 years, we've been trained that "output" means a Word doc, an Excel sheet, or a PowerPoint. Those formats describe things — they don't do anything. Here are 7 signs your documents should be doing more.

1

Calculation

There's math involved. Someone is pulling out a calculator or building a formula. → Build a calculator

2

Scoring

You're rating, ranking, or grading something against criteria. → Build an auto-scorer

3

Branching

There's if-then logic — "if the client says X, do Y." → Build a decision tree

4

Repetition

You're doing the same thing for every client, every time. → Build a template

5

Sequence

There are ordered steps that must happen in order. → Build a wizard

6

Input

You're collecting information from someone before you can act. → Build a smart form

7

Visualization

The data would be clearer as a picture than as text. → Build a diagram or dashboard

🔎 Scan Your Own Work

1

Open your last 5 deliverables — proposals, reports, emails, spreadsheets. Anything you sent to a client or your team this week.

2

For each one, ask: "Does this contain any of the 7 signals?" Calculation? Scoring? Branching? Repetition? Sequence? Input? Visualization?

3

For every "yes" — that document is a candidate to become a tool. A tool that works while you sleep, serves unlimited users, and never needs you to explain it.

4

Ask the guiding question: "What would this look like if it were interactive?" If you can answer it, you've found an upgrade.

"We've been using a spaceship to deliver mail. The same AI that writes your documents can build tools that calculate, decide, guide, and scale."

How This Plays Out in Practice

Say your team builds an SOP for "Processing a New Service Request" in Week 2 of the sprint. Here's what happens in Week 4:

1

Golden Example

An actual completed service request — the best one your team has ever processed. Not a description of what "good" looks like. The real thing.

2

Context Compiler

Pricing rules, service tiers, customer history, compliance requirements, SLA timelines — everything the agent needs to process requests the way your best person would.

3

QC Doc

Before we build: "A properly processed request must include X, Y, Z. It must route to the right team within N hours. It must flag exceptions A, B, C." The test script is written first.
